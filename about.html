Background

A Green Canvas

Introduction

The EPOCH 45 competition acts as a catalyst for reimagining entrenched urban paradigms within the dynamic landscape of IKN. Traditional planning approaches clash with the complex reality of developing a capital city on what is effectively a blank canvas. Urbanism's core tenets—context sensitivitxy, community engagement, and data-informed decisions—face a formidable challenge in crafting a future city on the green canvas of a monoculture tree plantation, devoid of history, intricate natural elements, or an established community. Participants are thus confronted with the persisting traps and temptations of designing the blueprints for a wholly engineered urbanity.

However, there are loopholes to navigate the inherent obstacles within the new town model. The theme of Evolutionary Planning invites exploration of a phased reality, accreting complexity over time to create its own critical mass, its own data, its own agro-ecology, sociospatial context, and ultimately, its own path dependence. No longer a static blueprint, the planning challenge extends, as teams are tasked with designing the spatial framework of a "forest city," a concept still open to interpretation, which in turn evokes more questions and introduces significant new paradoxes:

Spatial
How can a jungle be cultivated by planning a metropolis on the site of a tree plantation?
How can a vibrant and equitable city emerge from an existing urban core dominated by offices and exclusive housing compounds?
Is it possible for a metropolis to incorporate the aspired 60% forest while retaining spatial integrity, compactness, and walkability?
In the face of diverse risks and natural disasters including drought, flooding, heatwaves, typhoons, and wildfires, could nature-based solutions ensure the resilience of a forest city?
In a capital city planned in remote isolation, to what extent can its operations be virtual and cloud-based, and what is the physical infrastructure that would still be required?
Socioeconomic
Are the administrative and educational programs robust enough to support a city planned in isolation and promote its incremental growth and ongoing diversification?
What alternative business models can be explored if influx from global capital remains limited?
Can ecosystem services provide a sustainable foundation to support a thriving metropolis?
How can a sense of community be cultivated in a place where critical mass and long-term social cohesion need to be established from scratch?
Procedural
In what ways can incremental and adaptive planning models be effectively implemented in the planning and development of the new forest capital?
Who should be involved in participatory feedback loops and participatory budgeting?
What kind of management and policy structure are required to engage a diverse range of experts and stakeholders during a twenty-year iterative design process?
How to define and evaluate the distinguishing qualities of a forest city—a hitherto unseen concept? What are the KPIs and benchmarks to assess the success of its development?

Such narratives will escalate, while the competition advocates for concise, integrated visions to reinvent not only the new capital city, but also the novel planning methods it so urgently requires.
